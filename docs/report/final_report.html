Abstract

Customer-support contact centres in the telecommunications industry handle enormous volumes of repetitive interactions. Analyses of support traffic consistently show that two intents dominate: customers questioning or disputing a charge on their bill, and customers seeking a better plan or a discount. Both categories are latency-sensitive, rule-heavy and emotionally charged, which makes them simultaneously expensive to service with human agents and highly amenable to automation with modern Natural Language Processing (NLP). This project designs and implements an intelligent conversational Artificial Intelligence (AI) chatbot that provides automated, accurate and real-time customer support for exactly these two intents — billing-dispute resolution and personalized offer recommendation.

The system is built on a “thick brain, thin interface” philosophy in which the user interface performs only presentation while all language understanding, decision-making and action execution reside in a deterministic Python brain. Customer utterances are interpreted by a local, deep-learning-based Natural Language Understanding (NLU) stack comprising a Multi-Layer-Perceptron (MLP) neural-network intent classifier over Term-Frequency–Inverse-Document-Frequency (TF-IDF) features, a second MLP for sentiment analysis, and rule-based entity extraction. These signals are fused into a per-turn frame that drives a hub-and-spoke multi-agent dialogue engine: a coordinator (the hub) routes each conversational turn between a front-line billing-and-offers agent and a senior retention specialist, with the two agents never communicating directly. Responses are produced by a Natural Language Generation (NLG) module that pairs grounded response templates with an optional local Large Language Model (LLM, served by Ollama) for human-like phrasing, while strictly forbidding the model from inventing facts. Offer recommendations are ranked by a deterministic scoring engine and continuously improved by a reinforcement-learning bandit that learns which offers customers accept. Before any account data is served, the customer must pass a two-step identity validation.

The chatbot is delivered as a runnable Streamlit web application backed by a fully synthetic dataset, and is instrumented with live signals and Key Performance Indicators (KPIs) for monitoring. Evaluation demonstrates a sentiment classification accuracy of approximately 83% and an intent classification accuracy of approximately 62% on a compact held-out set (bounded by the proof-of-concept corpus size), a 100% resolution rate on the designed test scenarios, and low per-turn latency. The project fulfils all five stated objectives and provides a clear, modular foundation for a production system.

Chapter 1 — Introduction

1.1 Overview

The last decade has witnessed a decisive shift in the way organizations interact with their customers. Where once a telephone call to a human agent was the default channel for resolving an account query, customers now expect instant, around-the-clock, self-service resolution through digital text interfaces. This expectation has been amplified by the maturation of conversational AI: chatbots have progressed from brittle, keyword-matching scripts to systems capable of understanding intent, tracking context and generating fluent, human-like replies. This project situates itself at this frontier, focusing on the telecommunications (telecom) sector, where the sheer volume and repetitiveness of support requests make automation both commercially compelling and technically tractable.

The chatbot developed in this project is a text-based conversational agent that automates two of the highest-volume support intents in telecom: resolving disputes about charges on a customer's bill, and recommending personalized promotional offers. Rather than a monolithic script, the system is architected as a small society of cooperating AI agents governed by a central coordinator — a design pattern known as hub-and-spoke. The agent understands free-text input using locally trained machine-learning models, decides on an action using deterministic business logic, and communicates the outcome in natural language, optionally refined by a locally hosted Large Language Model. The entire system runs on a single laptop against synthetic data, making it fully reproducible and free of any privacy concerns associated with real customer records.

1.2 Background of the Domain

Telecom operators manage millions of subscriber accounts, each generating a monthly bill composed of a base plan charge, taxes, and a variable set of usage charges such as international roaming, data overage and premium add-ons. Because these variable charges appear intermittently and are frequently misunderstood, they are the single largest driver of inbound support contacts. A customer who travels abroad and returns to a bill inflated by a roaming charge, or one who exceeds a data cap and incurs an overage fee, will very often contact support in a state of confusion or frustration. Simultaneously, operators run continuous retention and up-sell programmes: loyalty discounts, data add-ons, family-plan consolidations and contract renewals, each targeted at particular customer segments. Surfacing the right offer to the right customer at the right moment is a recognized lever for reducing churn and increasing Average Revenue Per User (ARPU).

Historically, both of these workloads have been serviced by human contact-centre agents following Standard Operating Procedures (SOPs). This model suffers from well-documented weaknesses: high operating cost, inconsistent adherence to policy, long hold times that erode satisfaction, and difficulty scaling to demand spikes. The rule-driven, data-grounded nature of billing explanations and offer eligibility makes them ideal candidates for AI automation, provided the automation can understand messy natural-language input and act on the customer's real account data without fabricating information.

1.3 Problem Statement

The problem addressed by this project can be stated as follows: design and implement an intelligent conversational chatbot that, given a customer's free-text query, can accurately determine the customer's intent and emotional state, act on the customer's real account to explain or resolve a billing dispute or to recommend and apply a suitable offer, escalate difficult negotiations to a specialist agent, and do all of this in real time while never fabricating account facts and while continuously improving from customer feedback.

This statement decomposes into several concrete technical challenges: robust intent classification from short, colloquial utterances; sentiment tracking to drive empathetic and escalation behaviour; grounded natural-language generation that stays faithful to account data; a decision engine that encodes billing and offer policy deterministically; a mechanism for handing control between multiple agents; a learning loop that adapts recommendations to observed acceptance; and a usable interface that first verifies the customer's identity.

1.4 Motivation

Three motivations underpin this work. First, academic: the project is an opportunity to integrate a broad cross-section of the computer-science curriculum — machine learning, deep learning, natural language processing, reinforcement learning, software architecture and human-computer interaction — into a single, coherent, working system. Second, practical: billing disputes and offer recommendation are real, high-value problems whose automation delivers measurable business impact through reduced handling cost and improved customer satisfaction. Third, methodological: the project deliberately favours explainable, locally trained models and deterministic business logic over an opaque end-to-end Large Language Model, demonstrating that a hybrid design can achieve fluent, human-like interaction while retaining the transparency, controllability and factual reliability that regulated domains such as billing demand.

1.4a The Economics of Billing Contacts and Churn

A brief consideration of the economics clarifies why the two chosen intents are worth automating well. In telecom, the cost of acquiring a new subscriber is substantial, typically far exceeding the cost of retaining an existing one, so any interaction that reduces the risk of a customer leaving has outsized value. Billing confusion is a recognized driver of dissatisfaction and, ultimately, of churn: a customer who receives an unexpectedly high bill and then endures a long, unhelpful support experience is materially more likely to defect to a competitor. Conversely, a support interaction that resolves the confusion quickly, treats the customer fairly, and — where appropriate — proactively offers a well-matched discount can convert a moment of frustration into a moment of loyalty. The offer side of the system is the natural complement: surfacing a relevant retention offer at the point of contact, rather than through an untargeted mass campaign, both reduces churn and lifts revenue per user. Automating these interactions with an agent that is instantly available, consistent and personalized therefore attacks cost and churn simultaneously, which is the commercial rationale underpinning the technical work described in this report.

Chapter 2 — Literature Review and Background

2.1 Evolution of Conversational AI and Chatbots

The idea of a machine conversing in natural language is as old as the field of artificial intelligence itself. The first widely known chatbot, ELIZA, was created by Joseph Weizenbaum in 1966 and simulated a Rogerian psychotherapist through simple pattern-matching and canned transformations of the user's input. Despite its simplicity, ELIZA demonstrated that users would readily attribute understanding to a system that merely reflected their words back at them — an observation now called the ELIZA effect. PARRY (1972) followed, modelling a paranoid patient with a richer internal state. These early systems were entirely rule-based: they matched keywords and applied scripted responses, with no genuine understanding of meaning.

The subsequent decades saw incremental progress through better pattern languages such as AIML (Artificial Intelligence Markup Language), which powered award-winning bots like A.L.I.C.E., and through the rise of statistical NLP. The commercial inflection point arrived with virtual assistants such as Apple's Siri, Amazon's Alexa and Google Assistant, which combined speech recognition, statistical intent classification and structured back-end actions. In parallel, task-oriented dialogue systems matured in research, formalizing the pipeline of NLU, dialogue state tracking, dialogue policy and NLG. Most recently, the advent of large pre-trained transformer language models has enabled open-domain conversation of unprecedented fluency. The system in this project draws on this lineage selectively: it adopts the disciplined task-oriented pipeline for reliability, while borrowing the fluency of Large Language Models for the final phrasing step only.

2.2 Natural Language Processing Fundamentals

Natural Language Processing is the sub-field of AI concerned with enabling computers to process and analyse human language. A typical NLP pipeline begins with text pre-processing: tokenization (splitting text into words or sub-words), normalization (lower-casing, expanding contractions, removing punctuation), and sometimes stemming or lemmatization (reducing words to a common root). The cleaned text must then be converted into a numerical representation that a model can consume. Classical approaches include the bag-of-words model, which represents a document as a vector of word counts, and TF-IDF (Term Frequency–Inverse Document Frequency), which weights each term by how frequently it appears in a document relative to how common it is across the whole corpus, thereby emphasizing distinctive words. Modern approaches instead learn dense word embeddings (Word2Vec, GloVe, fastText) or contextual embeddings from transformer models (BERT and its successors) that capture semantic similarity. This project uses TF-IDF because it is transparent, fast, requires no pre-trained embeddings, and performs strongly on the short, domain-specific utterances characteristic of support chat.

2.3 Natural Language Understanding

Natural Language Understanding is the component of a conversational system responsible for converting a raw utterance into a structured, machine-actionable representation. Two tasks are central. Intent classification assigns the utterance to one of a fixed set of intents — for example, distinguishing “why is my bill higher this month” (a bill-explanation intent) from “I never went to Mexico” (a factual-dispute intent). This is a supervised multi-class text classification problem, commonly solved with algorithms ranging from logistic regression and support-vector machines over TF-IDF features to fine-tuned transformers. Entity extraction (or slot filling) identifies the structured arguments within the utterance — a charge identifier, a monetary amount, a date range, or a yes/no confirmation. Entity extraction is frequently handled with a combination of rules/regular expressions and sequence-labelling models such as conditional random fields or BiLSTM-CRF networks. The NLU stack in this project combines a neural intent classifier and sentiment analyser with rule-based entity extraction, a pragmatic and explainable configuration for a bounded domain.

2.4 Sentiment and Emotion Analysis

Sentiment analysis determines the affective polarity of text — typically positive, negative or neutral — and is a cornerstone of customer-experience analytics. In a support setting it serves two operational purposes: it lets the agent modulate tone (becoming more empathetic as frustration rises) and it acts as a trigger for escalation. Techniques range from lexicon-based methods, which sum the polarities of words found in a sentiment dictionary such as VADER or SentiWordNet, to supervised classifiers trained on labelled corpora, to transformer-based models fine-tuned for sentiment. Emotion analysis extends this to finer categories such as anger, frustration, satisfaction and disappointment. This project trains a compact neural sentiment classifier that outputs a three-class label together with a continuous signed score, allowing the policy engine to reason about not just the current sentiment but its trajectory across a conversation.

2.5 Machine Learning for Text Classification

Supervised machine learning frames intent and sentiment detection as learning a function from labelled examples. The classical workflow — feature extraction (e.g. TF-IDF), followed by a discriminative classifier — remains highly effective for small, well-scoped datasets. Logistic regression and linear support-vector machines offer strong baselines with excellent interpretability. Naïve Bayes, despite its independence assumptions, is a robust and fast text classifier. Ensemble methods such as random forests and gradient boosting can capture non-linear feature interactions. The key practical concerns are class imbalance, overfitting on small corpora (mitigated by regularization and cross-validation), and the need for a sensible fallback when the model is uncertain. This project adopts a neural classifier (described next) with an L2-regularized objective and a deterministic keyword fallback for low-confidence predictions.

2.6 Deep Learning and Neural Networks

Deep learning uses multi-layer neural networks to learn hierarchical representations directly from data. The Multi-Layer Perceptron (MLP) is the foundational feed-forward architecture: an input layer, one or more hidden layers of neurons applying non-linear activation functions, and an output layer. Trained by back-propagation and gradient descent, an MLP can approximate complex non-linear decision boundaries, and even over TF-IDF features it can capture interactions that a purely linear model cannot. For sequential text, recurrent neural networks (RNNs) and their gated variants (LSTM, GRU) model word order, while convolutional neural networks capture local n-gram patterns. The most significant recent development is the transformer architecture, whose self-attention mechanism captures long-range dependencies and underpins virtually all state-of-the-art language models. In this project the intent and sentiment classifiers are implemented as MLP neural networks; this satisfies the objective of deep-learning-based NLU while remaining lightweight enough to train in seconds on a CPU, a deliberate trade-off appropriate to a reproducible proof of concept.

2.7 Large Language Models and Natural Language Generation

Natural Language Generation is the task of producing coherent natural-language text from an internal representation. Traditional NLG followed a pipeline of content determination, sentence planning and surface realization, often using templates. The advent of Large Language Models — transformer networks with billions of parameters pre-trained on vast text corpora — has transformed NLG: models such as the GPT family, Claude, LLaMA and their derivatives can generate fluent, contextually appropriate text from a natural-language prompt. However, LLMs are prone to hallucination: confidently stating plausible but false information. In a billing context this is unacceptable — an agent must never invent a charge or an offer amount. This project therefore adopts a grounded, hybrid NLG strategy: deterministic templates carry the authoritative facts, and a locally hosted LLM (LLaMA 3.2, served through Ollama) is invoked only to rephrase those templates in a warm, human voice, under an explicit instruction not to introduce any new facts. This preserves fluency while eliminating the hallucination risk, and the LLM is entirely optional — the system degrades gracefully to pure templates if it is unavailable.

2.8 Reinforcement Learning and Multi-Armed Bandits

Reinforcement Learning (RL) is a paradigm in which an agent learns to make decisions by interacting with an environment and receiving rewards. Unlike supervised learning, RL has no labelled examples; instead, the agent must balance exploration (trying actions to gather information) against exploitation (choosing the action currently believed best). The multi-armed bandit is the simplest RL setting: an agent repeatedly chooses among several actions (“arms”), each returning a stochastic reward, and seeks to maximize cumulative reward. The epsilon-greedy strategy is a classic, easily interpretable solution: with probability epsilon the agent explores a random arm, and otherwise it exploits the arm with the highest estimated value. Contextual bandits extend this by conditioning the choice on features of the current situation. In this project, offer recommendation is framed as a bandit problem: each offer is an arm, a customer accepting an offer yields a reward of one and a decline yields zero, and the learned acceptance value is blended into the deterministic recommendation score so that offers customers actually accept rise in the ranking over time. This realizes the objective of continuous improvement from feedback loops.

2.9 Recommender Systems

Recommender systems predict the items a user is most likely to value. The two classical families are collaborative filtering, which recommends items favoured by similar users, and content-based filtering, which recommends items similar to those a user has liked, using item and user features. Hybrid and knowledge-based recommenders combine these with explicit business rules. In a telecom offer-recommendation context, pure collaborative filtering is often impractical because eligibility is governed by hard business rules (tier, tenure, plan type) rather than taste. This project therefore employs a knowledge-based, rule-plus-scoring recommender: customer attributes are bucketed into low/medium/high bands, offers are filtered by eligibility rules, and a weighted heuristic produces a score. This deterministic core is then refined by the reinforcement-learning bandit of Section 2.8, giving a recommender that is both explainable (every recommendation has a stated reason) and adaptive.

2.10 Dialogue Management and Multi-Agent Systems

Dialogue management governs the flow of a conversation: it tracks the dialogue state, decides the next system action, and maintains context across turns. Task-oriented systems often model each task as a finite-state machine or a frame-filling process. As conversational applications grow, a single monolithic policy becomes unwieldy, motivating multi-agent designs in which specialized agents handle different sub-tasks. The hub-and-spoke (or orchestrator) pattern is a widely used multi-agent topology: a central coordinator (the hub) receives every input, decides which specialized agent (spoke) should handle it, and mediates all communication, so that the spokes remain decoupled and never invoke one another directly. This yields clean separation of concerns, controlled hand-offs, and easy extensibility. The present system implements exactly this pattern, with a coordinator routing turns between a billing-and-offers spoke and a retention-specialist spoke, and it augments the flows with a lightweight policy engine that converts detected signals into tone and escalation directives.

2.11 Related Work — AI in Telecom Customer Support

The application of AI to telecom customer support is an active area in industry. Operators have deployed intent-routing bots that triage incoming queries, frequently-asked-question retrieval systems, and increasingly, generative assistants layered over knowledge bases through retrieval-augmented generation. Commercial contact-centre platforms provide sentiment monitoring, agent-assist suggestions and automated after-call summaries. Academic and industrial work has also explored churn prediction, next-best-action recommendation and dispute-resolution automation. The distinctive contribution of this project relative to that landscape is its integration: rather than addressing a single sub-problem, it combines identity validation, deep-learning NLU, grounded NLG, a knowledge-based recommender, reinforcement-learning optimization and a multi-agent hub-and-spoke dialogue manager into one coherent, runnable, observable system, while remaining fully local, reproducible and privacy-safe.

2.11a Comparison of Design Approaches

It is instructive to contrast the three broad design approaches available for a support chatbot, because the choices made in this project are a direct response to their respective trade-offs. The first approach is the purely rule-based bot, built from hand-crafted patterns and decision trees. Its strengths are total predictability, transparency and zero dependence on training data; its weaknesses are brittleness in the face of unexpected phrasing, an inability to gauge sentiment, laborious maintenance as rules proliferate, and a stilted conversational feel. The second approach is the fully generative agent, in which a single large language model both understands and responds. Its strengths are remarkable fluency and flexibility; its weaknesses are the risk of hallucinating account facts, the difficulty of constraining it to policy, its opacity, its latency and its dependence on a powerful (often remote) model. The third approach — the one adopted here — is a hybrid, modular design that uses learned models for understanding, deterministic logic for decisions, and a language model only for phrasing, under strict grounding.

The hybrid approach inherits the reliability and transparency of the rule-based design in the consequential parts of the system — intent handling, eligibility, credit and ticket decisions — while borrowing the fluency of the generative approach in the one place where fluency matters and errors are harmless: the surface phrasing of a reply whose facts have already been fixed. It also adds a dimension neither of the pure approaches offers cheaply: a learning loop that adapts recommendations to observed behaviour without retraining a large model. The cost of the hybrid approach is greater architectural complexity — more moving parts, more interfaces — but this cost is precisely what the disciplined subsystem decomposition of Chapter 4 is designed to manage. This comparison explains why the project does not simply wrap a large language model in a chat window: doing so would sacrifice the correctness and auditability that a billing domain cannot compromise.

2.12 Research Gap and Positioning

Two gaps motivate the design choices of this project. First, purely LLM-driven support agents, while fluent, are difficult to constrain and can hallucinate account facts — an unacceptable risk in billing. Second, purely rule-based bots are reliable but rigid and unnatural. The gap, therefore, is a design that captures the fluency of generative models and the reliability of deterministic logic simultaneously, while remaining transparent and adaptive. This project addresses that gap through its hybrid, grounded, hub-and-spoke architecture: deterministic understanding and decision-making for correctness, an optional LLM for fluency, and a reinforcement-learning loop for adaptivity. The following chapters develop this design in full.

2.13 Evaluation Metrics for Conversational Systems

Evaluating a conversational system requires metrics at several levels. At the component level, classification tasks such as intent detection and sentiment analysis are measured with accuracy (the proportion of correct predictions), precision (the proportion of predicted positives that are correct), recall (the proportion of actual positives that are recovered) and the F1-score (the harmonic mean of precision and recall, which balances the two and is especially informative under class imbalance). A confusion matrix exposes which classes are mutually confused. Because a single train/test split can be misleading on small corpora, stratified splitting and cross-validation are used to obtain stable estimates. At the conversation level, task-oriented systems are judged by task-completion rate (here, the resolution rate), the number of turns to completion, and latency. At the experience level, user-satisfaction is captured directly (for example through thumbs feedback yielding a Customer Satisfaction, or CSAT, proxy) or inferred from sentiment trajectories. This project reports metrics at all three levels: component accuracy/precision/recall/F1, conversation resolution rate and latency, and a satisfaction proxy, in direct correspondence with the fifth project objective.

2.14 Responsible, Explainable and Trustworthy AI

Deploying AI in a customer-facing, financially sensitive domain such as billing raises questions of responsibility and trust that a purely technical design cannot ignore. Three properties are particularly relevant. Explainability is the degree to which a system's decisions can be understood by a human; a recommendation that states its reason (“a loyalty reward for your long tenure”) is far more trustworthy than an opaque score. Factual reliability is the guarantee that the system does not assert falsehoods; in billing this is paramount, since a fabricated charge or credit could have legal and financial consequences. Privacy demands that personal data be protected and disclosed only to verified parties. The design decisions in this project are shaped by these principles: deterministic, reason-bearing recommendations provide explainability; the grounded-NLG discipline, in which the language model may rephrase but never invent, provides factual reliability; and the mandatory identity-validation step, together with the exclusive use of synthetic data, addresses privacy. In this sense the system is not merely functional but deliberately trustworthy by construction.

2.14a Ethical Considerations and Data Privacy

Automating customer support in a financial domain carries ethical responsibilities that extend beyond correctness. A support agent influences whether a customer is charged, credited or offered a discount, so fairness and transparency are essential: comparable customers should receive comparable treatment, and the basis of any decision should be explainable on request. Automated systems can also inadvertently encode bias if trained on skewed historical data, which argues for transparent, rule-bearing decision logic in the sensitive billing path and for careful curation of any learned components. Data privacy is equally central: customer accounts contain personal and financial information that must be protected, disclosed only after authentication, retained only as long as necessary, and handled in accordance with data-protection regulation. This project embodies these concerns by construction — it gates all account access behind identity validation, uses only synthetic data so that no real individual is ever exposed, keeps the consequential billing decisions deterministic and explainable, and confines the generative model to non-consequential phrasing. While a production deployment would require formal privacy impact assessment, consent management and audit logging, the proof of concept is designed to be extended in that direction rather than to work against it.

2.15 Software Engineering Practices for AI Systems

Building a reliable AI application is as much a software-engineering challenge as a modelling one. Good practice separates concerns into cohesive modules with narrow interfaces, so that the model layer, the business logic and the presentation can evolve independently. Configuration is externalized (here, into an environment file) so that behaviour can change without code edits. Deterministic components are favoured wherever correctness matters, with learned or generative components confined to where their flexibility adds value and their errors are tolerable. Every external dependency is given a fallback so that the system degrades gracefully rather than failing outright. Finally, observability is engineered in from the start: structured logging of each turn's signals and outcomes turns the running system into a source of evaluation data. These practices, drawn from the emerging discipline sometimes called MLOps, are applied throughout this project and are visible in its modular folder structure, its configuration-driven provider switch, its graceful fallbacks and its session logging.

Chapter 3 — System Analysis

3.1 The Existing System and Its Limitations

In the conventional telecom support model, a customer with a billing query or an interest in offers contacts a human agent by phone or through a live-chat window. The agent authenticates the customer, looks up the account in a Customer Relationship Management (CRM) and billing system, interprets the customer's problem, consults Standard Operating Procedures to decide on a resolution, and records the outcome. Where the situation exceeds the agent's authority — for instance, a large goodwill credit or a difficult retention negotiation — the case is escalated to a supervisor. While functional, this model exhibits several limitations:

  • High operating cost: human agents are expensive and support volumes are large and bursty.
  • Long wait times: queueing during peak periods directly degrades customer satisfaction.
  • Inconsistency: different agents apply policy differently, producing uneven outcomes.
  • Limited availability: staffed support is rarely truly 24×7 without significant cost.
  • Reactive offers: relevant promotions are seldom surfaced proactively at the point of contact.
  • Poor observability: the emotional trajectory and intent of a conversation are rarely captured as structured data for analysis.

Some operators have layered simple menu-driven or keyword bots on top of this model, but these are notoriously rigid: they fail when the customer phrases a request in an unexpected way, cannot track sentiment, and offer no learning mechanism. There is a clear need for a system that understands natural language robustly, acts on real account data, personalizes offers, escalates gracefully, and improves over time.

3.2 The Proposed System

The proposed system is a text-based conversational AI chatbot that addresses these limitations directly. After validating the customer's identity, it accepts free-text queries and, for each turn, understands the intent and sentiment, extracts relevant entities, decides on an action against the customer's synthetic account, and replies in grounded natural language. It resolves billing disputes by explaining charges and offering evidence-backed remedies (a provisional credit, a goodwill waiver, or a formal dispute ticket), and it recommends personalized offers scored against the customer's profile and refined by a learning loop. When a negotiation becomes difficult, a coordinator hands the conversation to a specialist retention agent that follows an escalating offer ladder. Throughout, the system exposes live signals and KPIs for monitoring. The key advantages over the existing model are instant availability, consistent policy application, proactive and adaptive personalization, structured observability, and zero marginal cost per conversation.

3.3 Feasibility Study

3.3.1 Technical Feasibility

The system is technically feasible with widely available, open-source tools. The NLU models are trained with scikit-learn and run on a CPU in seconds; the user interface is built with Streamlit; the optional Large Language Model is served locally by Ollama; and all data is stored as JSON files, avoiding the need to provision a database. The entire stack installs from a single requirements file and launches with one command, confirming that no specialized hardware or proprietary software is required.

3.3.2 Economic Feasibility

Because the project relies exclusively on free and open-source software and runs on commodity hardware, its development cost is effectively limited to time. In a production setting, the economic case rests on deflecting a proportion of human- handled contacts to the automated agent, whose marginal cost per conversation is negligible compared with a staffed interaction.

3.3.3 Operational Feasibility

Operationally, the system is designed for ease of use: customers interact through a familiar chat interface requiring no training, and the conversation is guided. For operators, the modular architecture allows the synthetic data adapters to be replaced with live CRM/billing connectors without altering the dialogue logic. The grounded NLG and deterministic decision core make the system's behaviour auditable, which is essential for operational acceptance in a regulated billing context.

The design is governed by four principles. Thick brain, thin interface: all understanding, decision-making and action live in the Python brain; the Streamlit interface only renders. Observability by design: each turn emits structured signals that the interface and the KPI aggregator consume. These principles reconcile the competing demands of fluency and reliability that Chapter 2 identified as the research gap.

The implementation follows a component-per-responsibility structure. The design favours simple functions operating on the shared state object over deep class hierarchies, which keeps the control flow explicit and easy to trace — an intentional choice for a proof of concept whose behaviour must be transparent.

4.9 Conversation State Design

All per-turn components read from and write to a single ConversationState instance. This object records the ordered list of turns; the intent and sentiment trajectories that let the policy engine detect a declining mood; the active spoke and hand-off reason; the current flow and step; the sets of offers pitched, declined and accepted; the list of executed actions; escalation flags; and the resolution status. Centralizing state in one object avoids hidden coupling between components and makes the entire conversation serializable to a session log for monitoring and evaluation.

4.10 End-to-End Conversation Walkthrough

To make the interaction of the subsystems concrete, consider the canonical demonstration conversation with the Bronze customer, Marcus Rivera, traced through the design. The customer opens with “why is my bill higher this month”. The presentation layer passes the text to the coordinator, which invokes the NLU subsystem; the intent classifier returns BILL_EXPLAIN with high confidence, the sentiment analyser returns neutral, and the entity extractor finds no specific charge reference. The policy engine, seeing neutral sentiment and no escalation condition, returns a default directive. The coordinator, on the billing spoke, routes the turn to the billing flow, which retrieves the bill from the data subsystem, computes the twenty-seven-dollar increase over the prior month, identifies the twenty-one-dollar overage line item, and returns a template stating the delta and the charge together with the grounded facts. The NLG subsystem rephrases this in Aria's warm voice — or returns it verbatim if no language model is configured — and the reply, along with the live signals, is rendered.

The customer responds “I never used that much data”. The NLU now returns FACTUAL_DISPUTE; the billing flow advances its state, fetches the metering evidence for the overage, and presents both the evidence and the tier-appropriate resolution options — for a Bronze overage, a one-time fifty-percent goodwill waiver or a formal dispute ticket. The customer then haggles: “that's too expensive, can you do better”. The NLU returns HAGGLE, and the coordinator's hand-off detector fires with the reason haggle. The coordinator swaps the active spoke to retention, records the hand-off, and produces, in one turn, Ray's bridging opening line followed by the first rung of the negotiation ladder — all mediated by the hub, with the two spokes never communicating directly. Over the next turns Ray climbs the ladder; when the customer finally accepts, the retention flow applies the offer under a simulated supervisor approval, the reinforcement-learning subsystem records a positive reward for that offer arm, the shared state's resolution status becomes resolved, and the monitoring subsystem logs the completed session for KPI aggregation. This single walkthrough exercises all eight subsystems and both spokes, and is precisely the journey captured in the recorded demonstrations.

Chapter 5 — Methodology by Milestone

The project was executed as six sequential milestones that mirror the standard machine-learning-application lifecycle: Data Collection, Data Preparation, NLU Model development, NLG Module creation, Reinforcement-Learning optimization, and Deployment and Monitoring. This chapter describes the methodology followed at each milestone and records its concrete outcome. Table 5.1 summarizes the outcomes.

5.0 Development Methodology and Process

The project followed an iterative, incremental development process rather than a single monolithic build. Each milestone was treated as a short cycle comprising design, implementation, verification and reflection, and the output of one milestone became the tested foundation of the next. This ordering is deliberate: data must exist before it can be prepared; a prepared corpus must exist before models can be trained; trained models must exist before they can drive generation; a working conversation must exist before a learning loop can be attached to it; and a complete brain must exist before it can be deployed and monitored. Because the brain was engineered to be independent of the interface from the outset, each intermediate milestone could be verified in isolation through the offline scenario driver, which kept feedback cycles short and defects local.

Version control with Git underpinned the process, and the codebase was organized so that the mapping between the design's subsystems and the source folders remained one-to-one throughout. Configuration was externalized early, so that experiments (for example, switching between pure-template and language-model phrasing) required no code changes. Reflection at the end of each milestone fed directly into the next: the numerical-stability issues observed while first training the classifier, for instance, motivated the regularization and corpus-enlargement decisions taken in the same milestone, and the perspective-drift observed when the language model was first enabled motivated the prompt-hardening carried out during the generation milestone. This tight loop of build-measure-learn, applied at the granularity of a milestone, is what allowed a broad system spanning data engineering, machine learning, generation, reinforcement learning and interface design to be delivered coherently.

5.1 Milestone 1 — Data Collection

Methodology

Because the project uses synthetic data by design, data collection meant curating a small but realistic dataset that could exercise every intended behaviour of the chatbot. Three representative customers were designed to span the loyalty tiers and the principal dispute types. For each customer, the following artefacts were authored: a profile (tier, tenure, plan, ARPU, segment, and the last-four identity digits used for verification); a current monthly bill broken into line items, with roaming and overage line items carrying structured evidence (tower identifiers, date ranges, metered usage); three months of payment history; usage history; and an interaction history capturing prior contacts and their emotional outcome. A catalogue of four promotional offers was defined, each with a target tier, minimum tenure, monetary value and applicability. Tier-based dispute policies were specified (which tiers receive automatic provisional credit and up to what limit). Finally, a ticket store was created to hold dispute tickets. All of this was captured as human-readable JSON and exposed through a single access module so that every other subsystem reads data consistently.

Outcome

A complete, self-contained synthetic dataset (data/synthetic_telco.json and data/synthetic_tickets.json) covering three customers, their bills with disputable roaming/overage charges and evidence, payments, usage, interactions, four offers, tier dispute policies and a ticket store — sufficient to demonstrate every use case end-to-end, with a clean data-access layer (app/data_store.py). No real customer data is used, so the system is privacy-safe and fully reproducible.

5.2 Milestone 2 — Data Preparation

Methodology

Data preparation focused on building the labelled corpus required to train the supervised NLU models, and on defining the text pre-processing that would be applied identically at training and inference time. A taxonomy of fourteen intents was defined to cover the target conversations — greetings, bill explanation, bill dispute, factual dispute, promotion inquiry, yes/no confirmations, haggling, ticket creation, ticket status, human-handoff requests, payment queries, farewells and an out-of-scope fallback. For each intent, a set of representative example utterances was hand-authored, deliberately varying phrasing to improve generalization. A separate three-class corpus (positive, neutral, negative) was authored for sentiment. The pre-processing routine lower-cases text, expands common contractions, strips punctuation while deliberately preserving the dollar and percent symbols (which are semantically important in billing), and collapses whitespace.

Outcome

A labelled corpus (app/nlu/dataset.py) of intent-tagged and sentiment-tagged utterances, together with a shared, deterministic pre-processing function (app/nlu/preprocess.py). The corpus is compact but balanced across classes and representative of the target domain, providing a reproducible basis for supervised training.

5.3 Milestone 3 — Develop the NLU Model

Methodology

The NLU stack was implemented as three cooperating components fused into a single per-turn representation. The intent classifier is a scikit-learn pipeline of a TF-IDF vectorizer (unigrams and bigrams) feeding a Multi-Layer-Perceptron neural network with two hidden layers and L2 regularization. The sentiment analyser uses the same TF-IDF-plus-MLP recipe with a single hidden layer, and returns a three-class label together with a continuous signed score derived from the class-probability distribution, plus an intensity. The entity extractor uses regular expressions and keyword matching to identify charge identifiers, monetary amounts, the dispute topic (roaming, overage, tax, plan) and yes/no confirmations. A fusion step combines these into a TurnFrame. To keep the system usable even before training, and to guard against confident errors on short inputs, a deterministic keyword classifier acts as a fallback whenever the neural intent classifier's confidence falls below a threshold. Models are trained by a dedicated script and persisted with joblib.

Outcome

A trained, persisted deep-learning NLU pipeline (app/nlu/intent_model.py, sentiment_model.py, entities.py, pipeline.py) that converts any utterance into a structured TurnFrame. On a held-out stratified split the sentiment model reaches approximately 83% accuracy and the intent model approximately 62% (limited by the compact seed corpus); at runtime the models are trained on the full corpus and backed by the keyword fallback, giving robust live behaviour. Metrics are reproducible via python evaluate.py.

5.4 Milestone 4 — Create the NLG Module

Methodology

Natural-language generation was designed around the principle of grounded rephrasing. Each dialogue flow returns a deterministic template string together with a dictionary of grounded facts (amounts, dates, offer titles, ticket identifiers). When an LLM is configured and reachable, the generator constructs a system prompt instructing the model to rephrase the template in the persona's voice, in the second person, without introducing any new facts, and passes the grounded facts as constraints. If no LLM is configured, or if the call fails, the template is returned verbatim. The LLM provider itself is pluggable through an environment variable, supporting a local Ollama model (the default), a pure-template mode, and hosted OpenAI or Anthropic providers for future use. This design gives human-like phrasing without sacrificing factual correctness or hard dependence on any external service.

Outcome

A working NLG module (app/nlg/generator.py, app/nlg/llm_provider.py) that produces fluent, grounded replies. With the local LLM enabled, replies are phrased naturally (for example, “We've charged your account a data overage of $21 for exceeding the allowed 3 GB, which is reflected in this month's total bill of $62. Does that look correct to you?”) while all figures remain faithful to the data. The provider switch and automatic template fallback were both verified.

5.5 Milestone 5 — Optimize with Reinforcement Learning

Methodology

Offer recommendation was cast as a multi-armed bandit problem. Each offer is an arm with a running count of the number of times it has been shown and the number of times it has been accepted; its value estimate is the acceptance ratio, initialized optimistically. When the recommendation engine scores offers, the learned value is blended with the deterministic base score, so that offers with a higher observed acceptance rate are nudged up the ranking. Rewards are collected from the live feedback loop: when a customer accepts an offer the corresponding arm receives a reward of one, and a decline yields zero; explicit thumbs-up/down feedback on replies is also captured. An epsilon-greedy exploration policy allows the system to occasionally surface a non-top offer to keep learning. The bandit policy is persisted to disk so that learning accumulates across sessions.

Outcome

An operational reinforcement-learning component (app/rl/bandit.py) integrated into the recommendation and dialogue flows, with a persisted policy (data/rl_policy.json) and a live view of learned acceptance rates in the interface. The feedback loop demonstrably shifts the ranking of offers over repeated interactions, realizing the objective of continuous improvement.

5.6 Milestone 6 — Deployment and Monitoring

Methodology

The system was deployed as a Streamlit web application organized as a small state machine of screens: an educational welcome page, a login screen, an identity-verification screen, and the chat screen. The chat screen renders the conversation using chat bubbles with a distinct avatar per persona, and a sidebar presents the customer context, live per-turn signals, the reinforcement-learning table, aggregate KPIs, and controls including the LLM-phrasing toggle and a session-save button. Monitoring was implemented by recording every turn's latency, intent, sentiment and feedback on the shared state, serializing completed sessions to JSON logs, and aggregating those logs into KPIs — resolution rate, average latency and a customer-satisfaction proxy computed from thumbs feedback.

Outcome

A runnable, monitored application (ui/streamlit_app.py, app/metrics.py) launched with a single command, with live signals and KPIs visible during a conversation and reproducible screen recordings of the full customer journey. The end-to-end system boots cleanly and passes the automated scenario tests described in Chapter 8.

7.3 NLU Implementation

The intent classifier is a scikit-learn Pipeline chaining a TfidfVectorizer and an MLPClassifier. At inference, the utterance is normalized, vectorized and classified; the predicted class and its probability are returned. When the top probability is below a confidence threshold, the classifier defers to a keyword rule set, preventing confident mistakes on very short inputs. The sentiment analyser follows the same pattern but additionally computes a signed score as the probability-weighted sum of class polarities, yielding a smooth value that the policy engine tracks across turns.

7.4 Recommendation Engine Implementation

The recommendation engine first derives the customer's bands: a churn proxy from the interaction history, a tenure band, and a usage band from recent data consumption. It then iterates over the offer catalogue, discarding offers whose tier or minimum-tenure eligibility is not met (and suppressing a Bronze overage waiver when there is no overage charge). Each remaining offer receives a weighted heuristic score that rewards higher churn risk, high data usage for data add-ons, tier match and the presence of an overage for waivers. The score is then passed through the bandit for learned re-weighting, and the offers are ranked. Each recommendation carries a human-readable reason, supporting explainability.

7.5 Reinforcement-Learning Implementation

The bandit maintains, per offer, a count of shows and accepts in a JSON policy file. Its value estimate is the accept-to-show ratio with an optimistic prior of 0.5 for unseen offers. The adjust_score function blends this value with the deterministic base score using a fixed mixing weight, so that learning nudges without overwhelming the rule-based ranking. The record_show and record_reward functions update the counts as offers are pitched and accepted or declined, and the policy is written back to disk after each update so learning persists across sessions.

7.6 NLG and LLM-Provider Implementation

The generator receives the persona, template, grounded facts and policy directive. If no live LLM is configured it returns the template unchanged. Otherwise it builds a constrained system prompt and calls the provider abstraction, which dispatches to Ollama, OpenAI or Anthropic based on the environment variable, returning the rephrased text or None on any failure — in which case the generator falls back to the template. The Ollama path issues an HTTP request to the local runtime, requiring no API key. This abstraction makes the intelligence layer entirely swappable and non-blocking.

7.7 Coordinator and Flows Implementation

The coordinator's handle_turn function is the single entry point per turn. It runs the NLU pipeline, evaluates the policy engine, and — if on the billing spoke — routes the turn to the billing or offers flow and checks the hand-off triggers, swapping to retention when one fires. It then composes the reply through the NLG module, records the turn on the shared state (including its measured latency), and returns a structured response object consumed by the interface. The flows are implemented as small state machines that read and mutate the shared state, advancing a flow_step and appending audited action records.

7.8 User-Interface Implementation

The Streamlit application is a screen state machine (welcome, login, verify, chat) driven by session state. The chat screen renders each message with st.chat_message, attaching a persona-specific avatar, a hand-off banner when a hand-off occurred, ranked offer cards, a per-turn signal caption, and thumbs-up/down feedback buttons. The sidebar shows customer context, live signals, the reinforcement-learning table and the KPI panel. A subtle but important implementation detail is that dollar signs in reply text are escaped before rendering, because Streamlit would otherwise interpret a pair of dollar signs as LaTeX mathematics and corrupt the displayed amounts.

7.9 Configuration and Environment

All environment-specific settings — most importantly the LLM provider and model — are read from a .env file through a small configuration module. This allows the same code to run in pure-template mode for fast, deterministic demonstrations, in local-LLM mode for natural phrasing, or against a hosted provider in future, purely by editing configuration.

8.6 Challenges Encountered and Their Resolution

Several non-trivial challenges surfaced during development, and documenting them illustrates the engineering rigour applied and the robustness of the final system.

Rendering of monetary amounts. An early defect caused dollar amounts to display incorrectly: the interface framework interprets a pair of dollar signs as a mathematical expression and typeset the text between them as italic mathematics, so a reply such as “your bill is $62, up $27” rendered as garbled symbols. The resolution was to escape dollar signs in all rendered message text before display. This is a subtle presentation bug that would have severely undermined a live demonstration, and its discovery underscored the value of reviewing rendered output rather than trusting the underlying strings.

Perspective drift in generated language. When the local Large Language Model was first enabled, it occasionally rephrased the agent's reply from the customer's point of view — for instance producing “I exceeded my data limit” instead of addressing the customer. Because a compact model can lose track of speaker roles, the system prompt was strengthened to state explicitly that the model speaks as the support agent, to the customer, in the second person, and never as the customer. After this change the generated replies were consistently in the correct voice while remaining grounded in the supplied facts.

Numerical stability of the neural classifier. On the very small initial corpus, training the intent network produced numerical warnings and unstable probability estimates. Adding L2 regularization, increasing the training iterations and enlarging the labelled corpus stabilized training, removed the warnings and improved both held-out metrics and the quality of the live signals shown in the interface.

Latency of the optional language model. Routing every reply through the local model introduced multi-second delays that would make an automated demonstration appear to stall. This was addressed by making the language model strictly optional and non-blocking — the deterministic template is always available instantly, and the model merely enhances phrasing when present — and by recording demonstrations in both a fast template mode and a natural language-model mode so that each could be shown to advantage.

In every case the resolution reinforced a core design principle: keep the deterministic path authoritative and fast, treat the generative and learned components as valuable but fallible enhancements, and make the system observable so that defects reveal themselves.

Chapter 10 — Conclusion and Future Scope

10.1 Conclusion

This project set out to design and implement an intelligent conversational AI chatbot that delivers automated, accurate and real-time customer support for telecom billing disputes and offer recommendations. The delivered system meets that goal end-to-end: it validates the customer's identity, understands free-text queries through deep-learning NLU, resolves billing disputes with evidence-backed remedies, recommends and applies personalized offers, escalates difficult cases through a hub-and-spoke hand-off to a retention specialist, phrases its replies naturally via an optional local LLM while never fabricating facts, learns which offers customers accept through a reinforcement-learning loop, and exposes live signals and KPIs for monitoring. All five objectives are satisfied, and the system runs reproducibly on commodity hardware with a single command. The central methodological contribution is the demonstration that a hybrid design — deterministic understanding and decision-making, optional generative fluency, and adaptive learning — can reconcile the fluency of modern language models with the correctness and transparency that a regulated billing domain demands.

10.2 Limitations

As a proof of concept, the system has deliberate limitations. The NLU models are trained on a compact hand-labelled corpus, capping held-out accuracy. The data adapters are synthetic rather than connected to live CRM/billing systems. The supervisor approval in the retention flow is simulated rather than routed through a real approval channel, and there is no transfer to a human agent. The system is text-only and monolingual.

10.3 Future Scope

  • Corpus expansion: mine real, anonymized chat logs and support tickets to enlarge the labelled corpus and lift NLU accuracy substantially.
  • Transformer NLU: replace or augment the MLP classifiers with fine-tuned transformer encoders for stronger generalization.
  • Live back-ends: swap the synthetic adapters for connectors to production CRM, billing and ticketing systems.
  • Contextual RL: extend the bandit to a contextual policy conditioned on customer features for sharper personalization.
  • Approval & validation workflows: add a real supervisor approval channel and a proactive validation agent that confirms promised actions were executed, reducing repeat contacts.
  • Multilingual & voice: extend to additional languages and to a voice channel.
  • Retrieval-augmented answers: ground open FAQ-style questions in a knowledge base via retrieval-augmented generation.
